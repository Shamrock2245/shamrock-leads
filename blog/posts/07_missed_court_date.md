## It's the One Thing You Cannot Do

When someone is released on a bail bond in Florida, there is one absolute, non-negotiable rule: **you must show up to every court date**. Missing a court date — even accidentally — triggers a chain of consequences that can make a bad situation significantly worse.

Here's what happens, step by step.

## Step 1: A Bench Warrant Is Issued

When a defendant fails to appear in court (known as **FTA — Failure to Appear**), the judge issues a **bench warrant** for their arrest. This means:

- Law enforcement can arrest the defendant **at any time** — during a traffic stop, at their home, at work
- The warrant shows up in **national law enforcement databases**
- If the defendant is stopped in another county or state, they can be arrested and extradited back to Florida

A bench warrant doesn't expire. It stays active until the defendant is taken into custody or surrenders voluntarily.

## Step 2: Bond Forfeiture Begins

When the defendant misses their court date, the court issues a **bond forfeiture notice** to the bail bond company. This means the court is demanding the **full bail amount** be paid.

In Florida, the bail bond company has a **grace period** — typically **60 days** from the date of forfeiture (with possible extensions up to 120 days) — to locate the defendant and return them to custody. If successful, the forfeiture is **set aside** and the bond is reinstated.

If the defendant is not found within this window, the bail bond company must pay the full bail amount to the court.

## Step 3: The Indemnitor Is on the Hook

Remember the person who co-signed the bail bond? The **indemnitor** (co-signer) agreed to be financially responsible for the full bail amount if the defendant fails to appear.

If the bond is forfeited and the defendant isn't found, the bail bond company will seek to recover the full bail amount from the indemnitor. For a $25,000 bond, that means the co-signer could owe **$25,000** on top of the premium they already paid.

This is why being an indemnitor is a serious responsibility — and why it's so important to choose a bail bond company that actively monitors defendants and sends court date reminders.

## Step 4: Additional Criminal Charges

In Florida, **Failure to Appear** is a separate criminal offense:

- **FTA on a misdemeanor charge**: First-degree misdemeanor (up to 1 year in jail, $1,000 fine)
- **FTA on a felony charge**: Third-degree felony (up to 5 years in prison, $5,000 fine)

These charges are **in addition to** the original charges. So a defendant who missed court on a DUI charge could now be facing both the DUI and a felony FTA charge.

## What If You Missed a Court Date by Accident?

Life happens. People get the date wrong, have a family emergency, or simply didn't receive notice. If you've already missed a court date, here's what to do:

### 1. Call Your Attorney Immediately

Your lawyer can file a **Motion to Reinstate Bond** and request a new court date. If you have a legitimate reason for missing the hearing (medical emergency, wrong date, etc.), the judge may reinstate the bond without additional penalty.

### 2. Call Your Bail Bond Agent

Let your bail bondsman know what happened. This is critical — the bail bond company needs to know you're not fleeing, and they may be able to help coordinate your voluntary surrender or your attorney's motion.

### 3. Don't Wait

The longer you wait, the worse it gets. A proactive surrender and a reasonable explanation is far better than being arrested at a traffic stop weeks later.
