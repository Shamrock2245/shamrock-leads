## The Most Important Hearing You've Never Heard Of

If someone you love has been arrested in Florida, the **First Appearance hearing** is the first critical moment in their case. It usually happens within **24 hours of arrest** — and what happens there determines whether your loved one comes home or stays in jail.

Here's everything you need to know.

## When Does First Appearance Happen?

Under **Florida Rule of Criminal Procedure 3.130**, every arrested person must be brought before a judge within 24 hours. In most counties, First Appearance hearings are held:

- **Daily**, including weekends and holidays
- **Via video link** from the jail to the courthouse
- **In the morning** (typically between 8:00 AM and 10:00 AM, depending on the county)

The defendant does not need an attorney present, though having one can make a significant difference in the outcome.

## What Does the Judge Decide?

At First Appearance, the judge addresses three things:

### 1. Probable Cause

The judge reviews the arrest report to determine if there was **probable cause** for the arrest. If not, the defendant may be released immediately. This is rare, but it happens.

### 2. Bail Amount and Conditions

This is the main event. The judge considers:

- **The nature and severity of the charges**
- **The defendant's criminal history**
- **Ties to the community** (family, employment, how long they've lived in the area)
- **Flight risk** — how likely the defendant is to skip court
- **Danger to the community**
- **The defendant's financial resources**

Based on these factors, the judge will either:

- **Set bail at a specific amount** (cash or surety bond)
- **Release the defendant on their own recognizance (ROR)** — no bail required
- **Deny bail entirely** — for certain capital offenses or extreme flight risks

### 3. Appointment of Counsel

If the defendant cannot afford a private attorney, the judge will appoint a **public defender** at this hearing.

## Can Bail Be Changed After First Appearance?

Yes. If the defendant or their attorney believes the bail is too high, they can file a **Motion to Reduce Bail**. This motion is heard at a separate hearing, usually within a few days.

Conversely, the prosecution can file a motion to **increase bail** or **revoke bail** if new information comes to light.

## Types of Bail in Florida

The judge may set one of several types of bail:

- **Cash Bond**: The full bail amount must be paid in cash to the court
- **Surety Bond**: A bail bond agent posts the bond for a 10% premium
- **ROR (Release on Own Recognizance)**: No money required — the defendant promises to appear
- **No Bond / Hold**: The defendant cannot be released (capital offenses, probation violations)

**Surety bonds through a bail bondsman** are the most common option for felony charges in Florida.
