## Someone You Care About Needs a Bail Bond — And They Need *You*

When a friend or family member gets arrested, the call usually goes something like this: *"I need you to sign me out."*

But what does "signing someone out" actually mean? When you co-sign a bail bond, you become the **indemnitor** — and that's a role with real financial and legal responsibilities. Before you sign, you deserve to understand exactly what you're agreeing to.

## What Is an Indemnitor?

An **indemnitor** (also called a co-signer or guarantor) is the person who takes financial responsibility for a bail bond. You're essentially telling the bail bond company and the court: *"I guarantee this person will show up to all their court dates."*

In exchange, the bail bond company posts the full bail amount with the court, and the defendant is released.

## What Does the Indemnitor Pay?

In Florida, the indemnitor pays a **premium** of **10% of the total bail amount** for state charges and **15% for federal charges**. This is the bail bondsman's fee and is **non-refundable**, regardless of the case outcome.

**Example:**

- Bail set at **$5,000** → Premium is **$500**
- Bail set at **$25,000** → Premium is **$2,500**
- Bail set at **$50,000** → Premium is **$5,000**

Many bail bond companies, including Shamrock, offer **payment plans** for larger premiums.

## What Is the Indemnitor Liable For?

This is the part most people don't fully understand. As the indemnitor, you are **financially liable for the full bail amount** if the defendant:

- **Fails to appear in court** (skips bail)
- **Violates the conditions of their release**
- **Gets re-arrested while on bond**

If the defendant misses a court date, the court issues a **bond forfeiture**. The bail bond company has a limited time (usually 60–120 days in Florida) to locate the defendant and bring them back to court. If they can't, the full bail amount becomes due — **and you're on the hook for it.**

## What Are the Indemnitor's Responsibilities?

When you sign a bail bond agreement, you're agreeing to:

- **Ensure the defendant attends all court dates** — this is your primary obligation
- **Stay in contact with the bail bond agent** — if the defendant's address, phone number, or circumstances change, you need to communicate that
- **Help locate the defendant** if they fail to appear — you may be asked to provide information about their whereabouts
- **Pay the full bail amount** if the bond is forfeited and the defendant cannot be found
- **Notify the bail bond agent immediately** if the defendant talks about fleeing, gets re-arrested, or violates conditions

## Can the Indemnitor Get Off the Bond?

In some circumstances, yes. If the indemnitor believes the defendant is going to flee or is otherwise unreliable, they can **request to be removed from the bond**. This is called **surrendering the defendant**.

When you surrender a defendant:

- You notify the bail bond company of your concern
- The bail bond agent may arrange for the defendant to be returned to custody
- The bond is discharged, and your financial liability ends
- **The premium you already paid is not refunded**

This is a last resort, but it's your right as the indemnitor. A good bail bond company will help you through this process if it comes to that.
